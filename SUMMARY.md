# Thriving In Place — Key Concepts Summary

## Overview

**Thriving In Place** is a guide about **zoning reform** and how changing local land-use regulations can create more livable, affordable, sustainable, and equitable communities. Rather than relying on distant, centralized infrastructure or large-scale development, "thriving in place" means building capacity within existing neighborhoods through updated zoning rules that allow more housing options, support aging in place, reduce car dependence, and strengthen community connections.

---

## Core Concepts

### 1. What Is Zoning and Why It Matters
Zoning is a set of local government rules that regulate land use—defining where residential, commercial, industrial, and recreational activities can occur. Most U.S. cities have zoning that allows only single-detached homes on ~75% of residential land, making it illegal to build duplexes, townhouses, multiplexes, or accessory dwelling units (ADUs) in most neighborhoods. This matters because zoning dictates housing access, walkability, public transit options, and how communities shape themselves over time.

### 2. Upzoning — The Solution
**Upzoning** means changing zoning rules to allow greater development capacity—more units per lot, taller buildings (up to 4 stories), mixed-use development, and additional housing types. Key reforms include:
- Eliminating single-detached-only zoning (allowing duplexes, triplexes citywide)
- Permitting Accessory Dwelling Units (ADUs/garden suites) on residential lots
- Allowing townhouses and multiplexes in neighborhoods
- Permits mixed-use development around transit stations
- Removing minimum parking requirements

### 3. Benefits of Upzoning

#### Aging in Place & Family Connection
Upzoning enables seniors to stay in their communities by building ADUs or downsizing within existing neighborhoods. Adult children and grandchildren can live nearby, sharing care and support while maintaining independence.

#### Walkable, Connected Communities
Walkable, mixed-use neighborhoods increase physical activity, improve health outcomes, and strengthen social ties. People drive less, bike and walk more, reducing pollution and carbon emissions.

#### Healthier, Cleaner, Safer Streets
Reducing car dependence improves air quality, creates safer places to walk and bike, and gives everyone—especially older adults—more options for getting around without a car.

#### Housing Affordability & Supply
Building more housing types on existing land increases supply, which helps stabilize rents and mortgage prices. The "filtering" effect means that when new homes are built, older and more affordable units become available to lower-income households.

### 4. Equity Impact
Exclusive single-family zoning has historically been used as a segregation tool. Upzoning promotes equity by:
- Making it easier for low-income and minority families to afford housing
- Expanding options for working-class families, seniors, and students
- Creating diverse neighborhoods that welcome all income levels

### 5. Land Use & Environmental Impact
Compact, walkable neighborhoods make better use of roads and utilities, saving cities money. Upzoning protects farms, forests, and open spaces from sprawl by building more housing density within existing urban footprints. Single-detached zoning accelerates urban sprawl, consumes natural habitat and farmland, and increases overall energy consumption through car dependency.

### 6. How to Advocate for Zoning Reform

| Action | Effort Level |
|--------|-------------|
| Discover your neighborhood's zoning online (zoning maps, National Zoning Atlas) | Easy |
| Read about permitted uses in your zone | Medium |
| Contact your municipal elected official | Easy |
| Attend public meetings or hearings | Medium |
| Talk to neighbors about zoning | Easy |
| Join a local pro-housing/zoning reform group | Medium |

**Advocacy Do's:** Show up, ask others to join, make fact-based arguments, share personal experiences.
**Advocacy Don'ts:** Avoid disrespectful comments, don't be fooled by opposition framed as "environmental," don't rush the process.

## Case Studies

### Minneapolis (Minneapolis 2040 Plan)
- **Background:** Housing crisis with ~50% of renters and ~67% of senior renters paying >30% of income on rent
- **Key Reforms (passed 2018-2024):**
  - Eliminated single-detached-only zoning; allowed duplexes/triplexes citywide
  - Allowed 3-6 story buildings along transit routes
  - Mixed-use development permitted across the city
  - Eliminated minimum off-street parking requirements
- **Outcomes:** Building more housing, reducing car dependence, creating healthier neighborhoods with clean air
- **Lessons:** Durable change requires vision, technical expertise, community participation, and patience. Opposition weaponized environmental reviews (Minnesota Environmental Rights Act lawsuit caused a 5-year delay), showing the need for persistent advocacy.

### Toronto (EHON & Bill 23)
- **Background:** Severe affordability crisis; average 1-bed rent at $2,592 (2023), homelessness >7,000 people (2021)
- **Key Reforms:**
  - **Bill 23 (2022):** "More Homes Built Faster" Act — up to 3 units on most residential lots without rezoning; cutting development fees and bureaucracy
  - **EHON (2022):** Allowed apartments up to 6 stories on major streets; loosened restrictions for multi-plexes (up to 4-6 units); permitted Garden Suites citywide in 2022
  - Laneway Suites allowed since 2019
- **Lessons:** Residential support matters—showing up to consultations, council meetings, public petitions demonstrates real public backing. Small wins build momentum for larger reforms.

## The "Thriving" Framework

| Dimension | What It Means | Key Indicators |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|
| **Housing** | Accessible, diverse housing options in existing neighborhoods | Affordable units built, ADU permits, multiplex conversions |
| **Walkability** | Communities designed for walking, biking, and transit | Reduced car trips, increased active transportation, nearby amenities |
| **Affordability** | Housing costs that don't consume excessive income | Stable rents, filtering effect, reduced housing cost burden |
| **Equity** | Fair access to housing across all demographics** | Reducing historically exclusionary zoning, diverse neighborhood composition |
| **Environment** | Reduced sprawl, lower emissions, better land use | Protected open space, reduced energy consumption, compact development |

## Key Zoning Glossary Terms

- **ADU (Accessory Dwelling Unit):** A smaller independent living space on the same lot as a primary residence
- **As-of-Right:** Development that complies with all zoning codes and proceeds without special approvals
- **Missing Middle Housing:** Multi-unit housing between single-family homes and high-density apartments (duplexes, townhouses)
- **Mixed-Use Development:** Allowing residential, commercial, and office uses within the same area or building
- **Upzoning:** Changing zoning laws to allow greater development capacity (more units, taller buildings)
- **YIMBY:** "Yes In My Back Yard" — supporting new housing in your neighborhood

## Why "In Place" Matters

The core thesis is that people often move away from places because their needs aren't being met locally. By reforming zoning rules **in place**—allowing more flexible development on existing properties—we create communities where:
- People can age in place and stay connected to family
- Housing is affordable and diverse for all income levels
- Neighborhoods are walkable, reducing car dependence and pollution
- Small business developers can build missing-middle homes that serve real community needs

---

## Key Takeaway

> *"Thriving in place" means reforming local zoning rules so neighborhoods can accommodate more housing types (ADUs, multiplexes, townhouses), support aging in place, reduce car-dependent sprawl, and create accessible, walkable communities—not waiting for something else to fix the housing crisis.*

> **The most powerful call to action: Just Show Up.** Attend public meetings, speak at hearings, contact elected officials. Local officials respond when residents show up and demand change.
